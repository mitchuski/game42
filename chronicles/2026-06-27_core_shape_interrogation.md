# Chronicle — interrogating the core shape (is the tetrahedron right?)

**Date:** 2026-06-27
**Status:** interrogation / open decision. Prompted by: "why the tetrahedron in the middle — is
it right for agentprivacy interop, or just inherited? and the 2D idea where station 7 moves into
the core to make another hexad that rotates out in a spiral."

## What the core is now, and where it came from
game42's centre is the **stella octangula** — two interpenetrating tetrahedra — lifted
byte-for-byte from the soulbis `/star` core. Its meaning there is the **dual agent**: the
Swordsman ⊥ the Mage, the two reflections (`neg`, `bnot`) whose composition generates the whole
lattice. agentprivacy "always starts with two." So the tetra-pair is **inherited, and it is
load-bearing for one specific claim** (the dual-agent ⊥) and for **interop** (it's literally the
soulbis star the games converge into).

## The mismatch worth naming
But game42's own symmetry is **6 and 7**, not 4:
- 6 heptads (hexagonal frame), 6 axes = the stratum-1 basis of {0,1}⁶.
- 7 stations per heptad (the faculty cube minus the void).

A tetrahedron is 3/4-fold. The *fold* rescues the 6: six lifted flaps read as a **hexagram** (6
points) in plan, which does match the 6 heptads. So the **outer** target legitimately wants
6-fold/hexagram. The question is the **inner core**: a tetra-pair is not the natural home of a
6-axis structure. It's there for the dual-agent and for soulbis interop — not because the game's
algebra asked for it.

## The 2D clue: station 7 → the core hexad
The observed 2D behaviour is the tell. Each heptad's **station 7 (the keystone guide)** is the
one that "holds all three faculties" — and the idea is it **moves into the middle** and, with the
other five keystones, forms **another hexad (a hexapod = 6)** at the core, which **rotates and
spirals out**. That is the game *describing its own core shape*: the six guides are a **6-vertex
core**, i.e. an **octahedron** (6 vertices) or a rotating hexad — **not** a tetrahedron.

This is elegant: the octahedron's 6 vertices ↔ the 6 axes; it is the natural dual to the
6-fold/hexagonal body; and "the part that holds all three can hold the whole" (SPEC §2) maps to
the keystone being the one that ascends into the core.

## Options for the core
1. **Keep the stella tetra-pair only.** Pro: dual-agent ⊥, soulbis interop, 64-tetra grid
   resonance. Con: not 6-axis-native; the 6/7 symmetry lives only in the outer fold.
2. **Replace with a 6-vertex core (octahedron / rotating keystone-hexad).** Pro: 6-axis-native,
   matches the 7→core behaviour, the guides literally are the core. Con: loses the explicit
   dual-agent ⊥ and drifts from the soulbis star (interop cost).
3. **Dual-layer core (recommended).** Inner **keystone-hexad / octahedron** (6-axis-native, the
   guides spiralling in/out) ⊥ outer **stella star** (dual-agent + soulbis interop). The ⊥
   between a 6-form and the 2-reflection star is itself the Φ_agent × (the rest) story made
   geometric — two becomes six becomes forty-two. Keep the tetra for what it's for; add the hexad
   for what the game actually is.

## Provisional answer
The tetrahedron is **partly inherited, partly right** — right as the *interop anchor and the
dual-agent symbol*, wrong as *the sole expression of a 6-axis game*. The spiral visual (built
alongside this chronicle) renders the **keystone-hexad core** spiralling in and out of the six
groups, inside the star — so both readings coexist and we can *see* which one carries the game
better before committing. Treat the core shape as a **decision**, not a default.

**[Conjectural]** octahedron-as-core (6 axes ↔ 6 vertices); the dual-layer ⊥.
**[Architectural, unchanged]** the dual-agent ⊥ and the outer hexagram fold.

> two reflections made the lattice; six guides make the core; the keystone is the one that can
> rise because it already holds all three
